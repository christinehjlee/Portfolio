branding, ui, ux, ecommerce, web design

Ni.co is a website that features a curated collection of creative solutions for your small space.


The Problem

Renters of "microhomes" (or homes that are less than 500 sq. feet) struggle with outfitting their apartment with appropriate furniture. I wanted to create a space dedicated to these tiny home inhabitants so that they don't have to sacrifice quality of living and style over space.

My Role

As the lead designer for this project, I was responsible for everything under both UX (user research, user stories, usability testing) and UI (low fidelity mockups, high fidelity mockups, branding).

Toolbox

pen, paper, niice.co, balsamiq, sketch, illustrator, invision

Meeting our Users

Catarina


Who

Catarina Kincaid, a recent graduate and first-time renter

Based In

Boston, Massachusetts (Back Bay)

Wishlist

Budget-friendly, close to downtown, walking distance to grocery stores

Meet Catarina. Freshly minted with a BA degree in Economics, she’s ready to take her first steps in tackling “real life.”

Having always dreamed of living in the city on her own, she began to look for apartments that would be close to her new workplace. As a self-proclaimed introvert, she knew that she’d need to have her own space in order to enjoy the hustle and bustle of the city while still being able to come back to her own sanctuary.

After weeks of dealing with reality and realizing that she wouldn’t be able to land her dream apartment right out of college, Catarina finally settled for a 260 sq. ft. apartment in the heart of downtown. Since she is paying top dollar for her space (re: $6/sq. ft.), she wants to make use of every nook and cranny to fully take advantage of her new home.

As a new grad and young professional, Catarina has a tight budget. However, she never let that interfere with her interest in good design, as she seeks out functional pieces and receives joy from being surrounded by interesting art.

Brandon


Who

Brandon Alvarez, a Digital Marketing professional looking to invest in his home

Based In

Boston, Massachusetts (Seaport)

Wishlist

Modern amenities, luxury preferences, proximity to downtown

On the other side of the spectrum is Brandon,who like Catarina, is also a young professional. Nearing 30, Brandon is quite enjoying his life in the city, resisting the urge to settle down and move to the suburbs like most of his friends.

Now that he’s had some time to save up and has the financial flexibility to upgrade to a bigger space, Brandon has grown to prefer living in his 515 sq. ft. studio. Not only is he thoroughly enjoying life in his swanky new high rise, but his studio is just big enough to pass for a 1 bedroom, is comfortable, and is easy to maintain (re: minimal cleaning necessary). He has everything he needs, so why bother upgrading to a bigger space?

Since Brandon has decided to stay in a smaller space and is craving an upgrade from his trusty 10 year old Ikea furniture, he now has some capital to invest in some “grown-up” pieces for his apartment.

Problem

Now that we’ve met both Catarina and Brandon and got a glimpse of their lifestyles, we can begin to see how young professionals are living in Boston nowadays. With rentprices steadily increasing for 1 person households in the Greater Boston area, it is getting more difficult to find a space that’s large, close to the city, and affordable.


Because of this predicament, more and more young professionals are sacrificing space to be close to the city. One of Catarina and Brandon’s biggest struggle is finding inspiration and furniture for their tiny apartments. A quick Google Image search reveals futuristic homes that have hidden cubbies and pull-out walls that can transform a kitchen into a bedroom into a living room, yet many of these microhomes have been custom built with the space constraint in mind.

These solutions are not appropriate for renters like Catarina and Brandon, as they don’t have the authority to remodel their property. This leaves microhome renters with a hefty wishlist, yet no solutions for how to organize their space.

Approach

To start things off, I began by talking to potential Ni.co users such as Catarina and Brandon to get to the crux of the problem at hand. What other sites have they tried to look for small-space furniture? What did they like / not like about these sites? What apps do they enjoy using? What kind of design aesthetic do they gravitate towards? Are they casual browsers or scour the Internet with a specific item in mind?

At first, these questions may seem excessive, almost interrogative,but really understanding where the user is coming from and what they want to accomplish always helps with driving the design.

After getting a good handle on the problem at hand, I began to scope out the rest of the project. It looked a little something like this:

  • Create user stories and user flows
  • Branding (naming + logo development)
  • Low fidelity mockups + usability tests
  • High fidelity prototype (with interactions) + usability tests

Process

After boiling down all of Catarina and Brandon’s needs and wants, I was able to come up with some user stories and user flows to help guide the design process. Branding

Seeing that our target audience had a lot of interest and passion for good design, I wanted to make this notion acccessible in Ni.co’s brand. When thinking of urban living, I think of architecture—buildings that lend itself to geometry and structure. Something that will be around for a while.

However, things that are structural in nature, if not done right, can also seem too perfect and cold. I wanted Ni.co to be approachable and relatable to the young clientele it would one day serve. I thought back to my Disneyland class in college (yes, that was a real class) and how they only use round shapes for their protaganists, and channeled this thinking into the branding of Ni.co.


As for the name “Ni.co”, that came out of experimentation and concatenation, which is how you name any startup, ever. Luckily, Ni.co was born out of this exercise, and it falls nicely with the assistant-as-app trend that has been floating around the interwebs lately.


Solution

As someone who has found herself in Catarina and Brandon’s shoes, I can relate to their situation. Knowing where to start can be incredibly difficult, considering you have to worry about other things like etc. This is why I decided to create a handpicked collection of items, ranging from furniture to lighting to decor that would be suitable for a small space.

Places like Ikea and West Elm currently have dedicated small space living sections on their website, but there isn’t one space that caters to many different tastes and constraints. Brandon wouldn’t care for Ikea pieces, as he wants investment pieces and Catarina wouldn’t be able to afford the selection at West Elm. Ni.co attempts to combine the best of both worlds, creating a suitable offering for both of our target users.

This is why I introduced the idea of “Collections.” Every Collection has a theme, which would allow for Ni.co to accomodate different tastes and priorities. In addition to being able to cater to many different personalities, Collections would allow Ni.co to scale and grow with their users.


The other thing Collections managed to do is to show the items in context, thus also serving as inspiration for how you can set up your space. The difference here is that each setup would be designed specifically for a microhome, so that users could see exactly how to set it up in their own home.

Ni.co was a theoretical project that I worked on for one of my Bloc assignments, so this is where the story ends for now. I learned a lot about how to set up ecommerce sites that optimize for a browsing experience and was heavily influenced by some of my favorite fashion bloggers. As the Internet lends itself to quickly spreading information, it’s become easier for individuals to set up their own shops and blogs. This new wave of sites dedicated to a more niche shopping experience is an interesting one, as shop owners are able to communicate with their customers more.

This is where Ni.co comes in. Ni.co helps renters of small spaces find inspiration on how to decorate their space to maximize every square foot while also making the space livable and comfortable.
