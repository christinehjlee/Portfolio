Mail Merge Templates

ui, ux, web application

12 min read

An enhancement on Mail Merge that allows for more effective outbound prospecting


The Problem

One of Yesware's most popular features is called Mail Merge— a tool that allows high-volume prospecting reps to quickly reach out to potential customers. At the time, there was no easy way to easily share these Mail Merges with the teams, making it difficult for team membres to quickly share successful messaging. As the number of prospecting reps grew amongst our user base, the need to share these campaigns grew as well.

My Role

As the Lead Designer for the Mail Merge Templates project, I was responsible for conducting user research (), creating low-fidelity wireframes (15%), creating high-fidelity wireframes (15%), conducting usability tests (50%), and overseeing implementation ().

Toolbox

Sketch, Google Drive, Invision, Sublime Text 3, Litmus, Pen, Paper

Meeting our Target User

Brad—a Sales Development Rep at Skalaway*. Brad works at a SaaS company in the heart of the Silicon Valley and is responsible for qualifying leads for his sales team. He has around 8-10 other SDRs on his team at any given time, and while competitive in nature, loves collaborating with his teammates to brainstorm better ways to find and qualify leads.

* Company name changed due to customer confidentiality

What is Mail Merge?

Mail Merge is one of Yesware's most popular features. This tool was created for reps focused on high-volume prospecting to make it easier for them to reach out to leads using the same messaging. Rather than send 200 individual emails with the same email template, a rep could use Mail Merge to import their list of 200 prospects, insert the template, then Yesware would automatically send those emails from their inbox.

Mail Merge was a huge hit when it was first rolled out x years ago. Since then, the Mail Merge user base has grown and their needs have evolved. Many users have requested an easier way to reuse their Mail Merges, especially if those Mail Merges are performing well.

Upfront Challenges

The interesting challenge that I had to face up front was that this wasn't the typical product development project with the refined process. Instead of coming from the Product team, this project came through the Customizations team. This meant that:

  1. There were more stakeholders involved in the project, which meant increased communication would be needed.
  2. The scope and requirements had already been determined by another team, so I had to accomodate that.
  3. We were on a tight timeline, as we needed to deliver the project on time, as the customer paid a hefty amount to have this feature development accelerated.

From the design side, this proved to be incredibly challenging. Even though this was a Customization that 1 customer paid for, the plan was to release this feature to everyone shortly after. However, since the scope was already decided, how do we know that other customers are going to want the feature built this way as well? Who trumps whom in the end: the customer who is paying for expedited development or the rest of the userbase?

The timing also proved to be especially challenging. In a typical product development cycle, designers stay ahead by 2 weeks so that we have enough time to validate, iterate, and incorporate technical limitations into the design. However, we were all starting the project on the same day, which meant there were no validated designs to work with, and everyone had their own ideas of how to approach the solution.

Having said that, a couple challenges didn't stop us! We acknowledged them and did our best to mitigate where possible. I tried to incorporate the regular process but heavily condense it to fit our timeline (i.e. instead of scheduling 5 tests, I scheduled 3; instead of doing 2 rounds of iteration, I did 1). Nobody said that building software was easy, but our team was up for the challenge!

Approach

While the engineers began work on the data model and restructuring the underlying architecture, I worked closely with the Product Manager and the Custom Solution Specialist to understand the requirements. We worked quickly so that I could create some prototypes to put in front of users.

Many sketches of key scerens and user flows later, I converted these into high-fidelity prototypes. Having spent 2 years talking to customers, we've found that they respond best when they see a realistic version of the mockup instead of a wireframe-y version.

Testing with Users

I tried to schedule as many different people as possible, since we didn't get to do the formal user research at the start of the project. One of the places that I compromised on was hte user research phase. I figured that having a prototype to respond to would yield fast feedback, so this seemed like the optimal approach.

One of the most interesting revelations from the first round of user testing was that in addition to the need for sales managers to create and share Mail Merge Templates, the reps also wanted to have control over creating Mail Merge Templates. There seemed to be a natural tension between reps and maangers, as the former party wanted more control and autonomy over their outreach efforts, while the latter party wanted to help reps save time and closely guard the messaging being relayed prospects. In addition, the workflows of both managers and reps greatly differed, despite using the same feature within the same interface. There was an even split: Reps preferred version B while managers preferred version A.

Given the time constraint, there was no room for us to explore a joint solution. My recommendation was to choose 1 persona and continue moving forward, and I would develop the other persona's solution on the side. Given this started off as a Customization, we decided to optimize for the managers and revisit the rep experience.

Implementation

To help speed the implementation and reduce the back and forth, I helped create and code the assets in HTML & CSS to be used in our Style Guide. This way, the engineers just had to pull in the class name for an element and the styles would automatically be applied. Of course, there was some tweaking required, but we all determined this was a better place to start from than nothing.

Shipping & Monitoring Results

After releasing the solution to the customer, the initial reactions were positive. Apart from this, they requested several more enhancement such as error handling, inserting from the Lead Page in Salesforce, and etc., which all required some brief exploration of flows and visual design. Once we received the A-OK from them, we began to slowly release the feature enhancement to other Mail Merge users.

Even though we had optimized for the sales managers' workflows, we were pleasantly surprised to see the usage numbers shoot up. Nearly 30% of all Mail Merges were sent using a Mail Merge Template!

This just goes to show that if the pain point exists and it is strong enough, reps will flock to find a workaround and be willing to change their workflow.

Next Steps

I continued working on the rep flow, and seeing the traction of Mail Merge Templates makes me convinced that continuing to refine the reps' workflows will pay huge dividends.

For now though, the feature has been shipped out to 100% of Mail Merge users and the number of Mail Merges being sent via Templates is also increasing.
